# Sub-task 4: CRO Patterns
# Page structure and conversion mechanics of top sales agency websites

Research date: 2026-05-21

---

## Research methodology

Sources: B2B website strategy content from Windmill Strategy, Bop Design, Ironpaper, Purple Sales, and general B2B CRO editorials (Trajectory Web Design, WDG Agency, SaaS Hero). Specific agency site structures inferred from indexed title tags, brand descriptions, Clutch profile structures, and editorial third-party descriptions. Direct page fetches were blocked; patterns extracted from available content.

---

## Universal Converting Wireframe (Reference)

Hero > Social proof bar > Problem/Agitate > Solution > How it works > Case studies > Objection handling > CTA > FAQ > Final CTA

This is the baseline. What follows documents where sales agency sites conform, diverge, and miss.

---

## Site-by-Site Section Analysis

### Belkins (belkins.io)
Indexed structure based on navigation and indexed content:
1. Hero: "#1 B2B Lead Generation Agency" + primary CTA "Book a Call" + secondary CTA to case studies
2. Social proof bar: Client logos (major brands)
3. Services grid: Lead gen, appointment setting, SDR outsourcing, AI sales platform
4. Case studies: Specific metrics (150% increase in booked meetings, 30% faster close rate for Leadium comparison client)
5. How it works: 3-4 step process
6. Team/credibility section
7. FAQ
8. Final CTA

Divergence from universal wireframe: The Problem/Agitate section is minimal or absent. Belkins assumes the buyer already knows their problem (not enough pipeline) and goes straight to proof. This works at their brand authority level but is a mistake for lesser-known agencies.

---

### SalesHive (saleshive.com)
Indexed structure:
1. Hero: "B2B Sales Agency and SDR Lead Generation Platform" + "Get Started"
2. Social proof bar: Review stars and client count
3. Service categories: Cold calling, email outreach, LinkedIn, AI platform
4. Stats block: Pipeline metrics
5. How it works
6. Integration logos (CRM tools)
7. Case studies
8. CTA form

Divergence: Heavy technology emphasis (platform features, integrations) before social proof with client names. Buries the human element. Makes sense for their positioning as a hybrid agency+SaaS but loses service-business warmth.

---

### Sales Xceleration (salesxceleration.com)
Structure inferred from indexed content and sitemap:
1. Hero: "Outsourced and Fractional Sales Leadership" + "Find an Advisor"
2. Problem framing: Speaks to small business owners who need experienced sales leadership
3. How it works: Advisor matching model
4. Advisor profiles: Trust built through individual expert bios
5. Results/case studies
6. CTA: "Find a Sales Leader"

Divergence: Advisor-profile-first model. Trust is built through individuals, not brand. This is a marketplace pattern, not a service agency pattern. Works for their franchise/advisor model but does not translate to a single-team agency.

---

### Martal Group (martal.ca)
Indexed from multiple content pages and service pages:
1. Hero: Outcome-led + niche specificity (tech/SaaS positioning)
2. Stats bar: Clients served, industries covered, years in operation
3. Services
4. How it works (3-step process)
5. Named case studies with metrics
6. Integration and tool logos
7. Testimonials
8. CTA

Divergence: Strong alignment with universal wireframe. The notable addition is a "why outsource vs in-house" comparison block that explicitly handles the build-vs-buy objection. This is a conversion win most smaller agencies miss.

### O8 Agency (o8.agency - outsourced sales team page)
Indexed structure:
1. Hero: "Revenue-Driven Results - Outsourced Sales Team" + primary CTA
2. Services offered (SDR types, roles)
3. How it works
4. Social proof (case studies)
5. CTA

Divergence: The page is a service pillar page within a larger generalist agency, not a dedicated sales agency homepage. The lack of a focused social proof bar and the generic agency wrapper hurt conversion. No problem/agitate section.

## Observed Patterns Across All Sites

### Hero composition
- Background: Dark gradient or solid dark (navy, near-black) is the dominant choice for authority positioning. Some use white with bold typography. Stock photography of smiling teams is common but conversion-negative (generic).
- Primary CTA: Always one button, above the fold, action verb + low-friction noun ("Book a Call," "Get Started," "Build Pipeline"). Never more than 3 words.
- Secondary CTA: Case studies or "See Results" link, text-only, lower visual weight.
- Social proof in hero: Number stat ("1,000+ companies served," "4.9 stars on G2") placed below headline and above or beside CTA.

### Trust stack ordering
1. Stats (clients served, meetings booked, years operating) -- first, because they are scannable
2. Client logos -- second, brand familiarity transfers immediately
3. Review platform badges (Clutch, G2) -- third, third-party validation
4. Video testimonials or case study excerpts -- fourth, requires more investment to consume
5. Team photos/bios -- fifth, humanises after credibility is established

### Form patterns
- Most sales agency sites use a minimal form: First Name, Last Name, Email, Company, "Tell us about your goals" (one free-text field). 4-5 fields total.
- Multi-step forms (3 steps: contact > company > challenge) convert better than single long forms, based on B2B conversion data (cited in SaaS Hero editorial).
- Placement: Hero form (highest intent), sticky "Book a Call" button throughout, and repeated CTAs at bottom of each section.

### Sticky elements
- Sticky header with CTA button: Nearly universal across top agencies. Ensures the primary action is always visible on scroll.
- Sticky "chat" or "talk to us" widget: Common. Adds a lower-friction entry point for browsers.

## Where Sales Agency Sites Diverge from the Universal Wireframe

1. **Problem/Agitate is usually skipped.** Top agencies (Belkins, CIENCE, SalesHive) assume buyer awareness and jump from hero to proof. This works with brand recognition but is a conversion gap for any agency without established authority. The wireframe's Problem/Agitate section should be kept for smaller/newer agencies.

2. **Objection handling is buried in FAQ.** Most sites address "will you understand our market?" only in a FAQ block near the footer. Given that this is the #1 buyer fear (Sub-task 2), objection handling should appear earlier -- ideally as a dedicated section between How It Works and Case Studies.

3. **Case studies are short.** Most agencies show a metric (150% increase in meetings) with a one-line client quote. Full narrative case studies (problem > approach > result) are rare but convert better for high-ticket services. Buyers making a $5,000-$15,000/month decision want more depth.

4. **Social proof bar is often logo-only.** Many sites show logos without context. "We worked with these 40 companies" is weaker than "Here's what the CMO of [Company] said after 90 days." Named quotes beside logos outperform logos alone.

5. **No risk reversal section.** Almost no sales agency website has a dedicated "what happens if we don't deliver" section. This is a large missed conversion opportunity given buyer fear #2 (paying for months and getting nothing).
